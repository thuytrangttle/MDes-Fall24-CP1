    This was the reason I became so invested in learning about design systems. It challenges me to go beyond just solving immediate design problems and instead focus on creating flexible solutions that can adapt across products. In the long run, it builds consistency and saves time.
"The concept of a grid system has so far served two purposes. First, it is the byproduct of the technical manufacturing processes of design products. The printing press works by assembling small rectangular letter blocks on a plate, so the designer has to think about the design process in terms of a grid. In this case, the grid is a concrete limitation that designers have to work around. Secondly, grid systems are also a design methodology used by designers to achieve organized layouts even in places that do not have such technical limitations.""
    The origins of grid systems in printing illustrate how technology shapes design practices. The necessity to align typeset letters on a grid reflects the limitations of early printing methods, forcing designers to adopt a structured approach. This is aligned with the notion that designer's must learn technical skills to remain relavant. While grids offer a foundation, they are not rigid rules. Designers often adapt or break grids to achieve specific artistic goals or to reflect the brand identity.

What Do Prototypes Prototype?

"If the role is well understood, but the goal of the artifact is to present its functionality in a novel way, then prototyping must focus on how the artifact will look and feel. If the artifact's func- tionality is to be based on a new technique, ques- tions of how to implement the design may be the focus of prototyping efforts."
    This is significant because it highlights two main ways of prototyping: design exploration and technical implementation. If the goal is to refine the user experience or visual design, prototypes should focus on the look and feel of the product. If the goal is to test functionality or new technology, prototyping should prioritize how things work.
"Clarifying what aspects of a prototype correspond to the even- tual artifact—and what don't—is a key part of suc- cessful prototyping."
    t's not always obvious how a prototype correspnds to the final product. It takes many converations with between design and dev team to make sure there are no misunderstandings and that edge cases and limitations are accounted for. It's easy to desgn in a siloe anf end up wasting time and effort effort on irrelevant features and misaligned expectations.

    This is a great reminder that as designers and product builders, we often get comfortable with our shorthand—using abstract, unfinished prototypes to convey ideas. But it's easy to forget that outside stakeholders or users won't have that same context.
